abstract: 
Machine learning (ML) has emerged as a transformative tool for simulating complex systems in physics. Particle collisions in collider experiments, such as those at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN, are modeled using Monte Carlo Event Generators (MCEGs) like Pythia—tools relied upon by researchers worldwide to simulate the entire collision process. A critical challenge within MCEGs is hadronization—the process by which quarks and gluons combine into hadrons (e.g., protons), which are subsequently detected. Hadronization cannot be described from first principles and relies on empirical models that have remained largely unchanged for over four decades, often failing to fully capture the underlying physics.

In this talk, I will introduce MLHAD, a novel ML-based framework that integrates generative models, such as Normalizing Flows, into the simulation pipeline to replace or correct these traditional approaches. MLHAD generates fine-grained, particle-level information beyond experimental measurements, that could significantly enhancing simulation accuracy and computational efficiency. These advancements benefit both experimental efforts, such as those conducted by ATLAS, CMS, LHCb, DUNE, and MicroBooNE, and theoretical studies, providing more accurate and adaptable tools for modeling and analysis. Additionally, they address broader ML challenges involving the modeling of unobservable, fine-grained information.
